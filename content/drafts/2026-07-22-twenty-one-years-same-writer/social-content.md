## X thread

1/ I ran 21 years of my writing through an anti-slop checker. 2005 me scored 2. 2026 me scored 2. An AI-assisted newsletter sent under my name scored 1. The machine was cleaner than I was.

2/ We recovered 1,274 old blog posts from the Wayback Machine. The useful voice corpus contains 851 posts and 147,893 words after comments, blockquotes, and tiny link drops were removed.

3/ The first experiment looked like human beats machine: unsent agent draft, score 20. Shipped newsletter, score 0. Then the provenance check blew it up. The sent version was mostly AI words too.

4/ The real comparison was edited versus unedited. The checker caught the model when nobody fought it. Once a person rewrote and scrubbed it, the score dropped to zero.

5/ That means an anti-slop gate can improve writing. It cannot prove authorship. It finds lazy machine habits, not the human.

6/ We also retracted a rhythm finding. The statistic measured variation between hundreds of posts, not rhythm within a post. A gate based on it would have flagged my own writing as robotic.

7/ Two possible tells survived as questions, not proof: unguarded me swears about 7x more and says “I” about 7x more than writing produced on my behalf. The register is different, so test before shipping a gate.

8/ The archive is the weird treasure here. Pre-AI writing has provenance by date. It is a great style source and a terrible knowledge source. Train voice on it. Never train facts on it.

9/ A checker can remove the slop. It cannot add a person. If your system cannot answer who wrote, changed, and approved the words before the score appears, you have lint, not provenance. [LINK]
