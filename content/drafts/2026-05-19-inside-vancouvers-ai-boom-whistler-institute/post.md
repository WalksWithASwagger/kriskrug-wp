Vancouver's AI story is not only a story about labs, funding announcements, and startup decks.

It is also a story about VFX artists, game studios, educators, civic leaders, open-source builders, journalists, Indigenous protocol, public meetups, weird little demos, and hundreds of people showing up after work because they know this technology is going to change their jobs, their classrooms, their culture, and their communities.

That is the story I tried to tell at the Whistler Institute.

The public question was simple: why does Vancouver's AI boom matter?

My answer is that BC has a real shot at being useful to the world here, but not if we treat AI as only a technical race. The regional advantage is creative, communal, ethical, and practical. It lives in the overlap between people who build things, people who make culture, people who teach, people who govern, and people who are willing to learn in public.

## The Boom Is Bigger Than The Tech Sector

When people talk about AI ecosystems, they usually jump straight to labs, capital, and founders.

Those things matter. But in BC, the creative economy matters just as much. Film, VFX, animation, games, digital art, design, festivals, journalism, and community media have already trained this region to work across technology and culture.

That matters because AI is not just another software category. It is a cultural technology. It changes what people can make, what organizations can automate, what students can outsource, what artists can prototype, and what institutions can get wrong at scale.

If you only understand the model, you miss the room it lands in.

## Vancouver AI As A Public Learning Lab

The Vancouver AI community has become one of the strongest proof points in this story.

The meetups are not just networking nights. They are public practice. People bring prototypes, fears, questions, half-baked workflows, policy concerns, creative experiments, and hard-won lessons from real deployments.

That kind of community infrastructure is underrated. It gives people a trusted place to learn before the stakes get higher. It creates shared language across industries. It helps founders hear from educators, artists hear from engineers, and civic leaders hear from people who are already using the tools in messy real life.

In a fast-moving AI ecosystem, that is not soft stuff. That is resilience.

## Ethics Cannot Be Bolted On Later

The easy version of AI ethics is a panel, a checklist, and a policy PDF no one reads.

The harder version is changing how organizations make decisions. Who is in the room? What data is being used? What rights do creators have? What oversight exists? What happens when a system fails? Who has the power to say no?

BC can contribute something important here if we stop treating ethics as a decorative layer. We need ethical relationships, ethical institutions, and ethical deployment practices. We need people who can translate between technical systems and community consequences.

That is one of the reasons BC + AI exists.

## Why It Matters For Event Organizers

This talk works for civic audiences, business audiences, creative industries, education, tourism, policy rooms, and regional economic development groups because it connects the dots.

AI is not one conversation anymore. It is workforce, culture, governance, education, media, creativity, infrastructure, sovereignty, and trust.

The useful speaker is the one who can move between those rooms without reducing the stakes to either techno-optimism or institutional fear.

That is the work I am trying to do.
