AI is doing two things at once.

It is built on a pile of work that artists, photographers, designers, writers, coders, and musicians did not meaningfully consent to hand over. It is also giving some of those same people the most intense creative leverage they have ever had.

Pretending only one of those things is true is how the whole conversation gets stupid.

This LaSalle College keynote is the clearest version of my Both Hands Full talk so far: a practical, messy, honest frame for creative people who are trying to keep their integrity while learning how to work with the most powerful creative tools of our lifetimes.

It is not a hype talk. It is not a doom talk. It is a room for the contradiction.

The Lazy Binary Is Not Enough

The AI conversation keeps trying to force creative people into two teams.

One side says the tools are inevitable, resistance is pointless, and artists should stop complaining and learn to prompt. The other side says the whole thing is theft, collapse, and cultural ruin, so engaging with it at all is a kind of surrender.

I understand both impulses. I have felt both in my own body.

My photography has been on the open web for decades. My voice, images, writing, and ideas are part of the training soup now. That is not abstract to me. At the same time, I have never been more productive, more curious, or more able to prototype the things in my head.

That is why the talk is called Both Hands Full.

What Both Hands Full Means

Both hands full is not compromise. It is capacity.

One hand holds the grief, anger, copyright mess, labor disruption, bias, environmental cost, and surveillance risk.

The other hand holds creative agency, new tools, small-team power, weird new workflows, accessibility, experimentation, and the possibility that artists can shape the next layer of culture instead of only being shaped by it.

The work is learning to walk without dropping either side.

For creative professionals, that means the question is no longer simply, "Should I use AI?" The better questions are:

  • What part of my process actually benefits from machine help?
  • What part of my work must stay human, embodied, relational, or handmade?
  • What is my taste, and can I articulate it well enough to direct these tools?
  • What parts of my archive, voice, and creative DNA am I willing to train on?
  • What boundaries do I need before I put client, student, community, or personal material into a system?

Those are better questions than "AI good?" or "AI bad?"

The Creative Skill Is Moving Upstream

The cheap part is generation now. Images, drafts, summaries, riffs, variants, mockups - the machine can spray adequate everything all day.

The valuable part is judgment.

Can you tell what is good? Can you tell what is yours? Can you tell when the output has no soul, no context, no risk, no taste? Can you build a workflow where the tool expands you without flattening you?

This is why I keep telling creative rooms that self-knowledge is becoming a technical skill. The better you know your taste, values, influences, constraints, voice, and desired impact, the better you can use these systems without being absorbed by them.

If you do not know what you are trying to make, the machine will happily make something average for you.

Why This Talk Belongs In Creative Schools And Cultural Rooms

Creative students do not need another shallow tool demo.

They need language for the ethical mess. They need permission to feel conflicted. They need concrete practices for protecting sensitive work and building personal creative systems. They need to hear from someone who has lived through several waves of technological disruption and still believes in artists.

That is what this keynote is for.

It works for design schools, art schools, film programs, agencies, studios, festivals, museums, media teams, and any organization trying to help creative people adapt without becoming cynical or obedient.
