Some talks are built for a ballroom.

This one was built for a festival brain stage.

`Dear AI, We Need To Talk About Your Soul` drops into a different kind of room: artists, teachers, coders, ravers, skeptics, builders, and curious people trying to figure out whether machine intelligence can still belong to human culture.

The point was not to make AI feel safe. The point was to make the conversation honest enough that people could stay in it.

## A Thinking Room, Not A Slide Deck

The Bass Coast session matters because it is participatory.

It is not a polished executive keynote with every edge sanded smooth. It is a live public thinking room where people bring anxiety, curiosity, ethics, creative practice, and real questions about what happens next.

That is a different speaking skill.

Some audiences need a clean keynote. Some need a workshop. Some need a guided conversation that can hold uncertainty without collapsing into vibes.

This session sits in that third category.

## Consent, Creativity, And Smaller Models

One of the strongest threads in the talk is consent.

Artists do not have to accept a future where all culture becomes raw material for giant systems they do not control. Small models, creator-owned datasets, local tools, and consent-based training are part of the alternative.

That does not solve every problem, but it changes the posture.

Instead of asking only what the largest platforms will do to artists, we can also ask what artists, communities, schools, festivals, and cultural organizations can build for themselves.

## Keeping AI Attached To Human Meaning

The soul question is not mystical fluff.

It is about whether AI systems stay connected to laughter, grief, longing, weirdness, memory, care, and the human reasons people make things in the first place.

If the tools only optimize production, they will flatten culture. If they help people explore, reflect, connect, remix, preserve, and make meaning, then there is still room to fight for a better version.

That is why this talk belongs beside the more formal keynotes. It shows the wilder edge of the work.
