Introduction:

  • Start with the question that began it all: You were driving around, thinking about how people talk about AI art as lacking something, and that something felt tied to the human experience. Reflect on how, as a photographer, you've spent 20 years honing an eye for light, contrast, and nuance, something that isn’t just technique but deeply tied to your lived, embodied experience.
  • Introduce the idea that this piece isn’t about final answers or a clear stance on AI but rather an exploration of how humanity and technology are smudging together, and what that means for the creative soul.

Section 1: The Body Knows-Embodiment in the Creative Process

  • Begin with a philosophical reflection: What does it mean to create from the body? Draw on Merleau-Ponty’s ideas about perception and embodiment, how our understanding of the world is shaped by being physically present in it, not just intellectually aware.
  • Share your personal experience: As a photographer, you’ve come to know light, not just by studying it but by feeling it in your bones after years of practice. It's this embodied wisdom that allows you to pull out your camera at exactly the right moment.
  • Set the stage: AI, for all its capabilities, can’t have that lived, embodied experience. It can analyze light, recognize patterns, and spit out results, but it can’t feel the sun on its skin or the emotional weight of a fleeting moment.

Section 2: Where AI Enters-A Tool, Not a Replacement

  • Shift to AI’s role. Acknowledge the power of AI: it can process data at speeds and scales unimaginable to humans, and in many ways, it can mimic creativity. But, as you mused earlier, it’s creating ideas for a painting or photograph, not living the life of a painter or photographer.
  • Introduce Walter Benjamin’s concept of “aura”, the uniqueness of a work of art that comes from its place in time and space. AI can’t give art that historical or emotional context; it creates images without life behind them.
  • Nuance the conversation: AI isn’t a threat, but it isn’t a savior either. It’s a tool, a collaborator that, in the right hands, can elevate a human’s vision, but only if it remains tethered to that embodied, human experience.

Section 3: Smudging the Lines-The Blurring Between Human and Machine

  • Now, dig deeper into the philosophical exploration. Where do humanity and technology intersect in the creative process? We live in an age where these lines are getting blurry. AI doesn’t just generate art, it alters how we think about creativity itself.
  • Discuss how AI can enhance or extend human creativity, but it also raises questions: If AI can generate art without emotion or experience, what does that say about our understanding of what art is? What’s lost or gained when the process becomes collaborative between human and machine?
  • Bring in Jaron Lanier’s thoughts on the humanizing of technology and how AI can amplify human creativity rather than replace it, but also acknowledge that there are dangers in treating AI as a stand-in for human experience. We risk losing that embodied connection.

Section 4: The Drawbacks and the Potential-Embracing Complexity

  • Here’s where you reflect on the drawbacks. AI can generate an endless stream of ideas, but at what cost? Talk about the risks: losing the human touch, over-reliance on machine-generated outputs, and the temptation to let AI do the heavy lifting.
  • At the same time, there’s potential here. AI can free up creatives from tedious tasks, allowing them to focus on the bigger picture. It can offer new perspectives, push creative boundaries, and make previously impossible projects a reality.
  • But, ultimately, you return to the same point: AI is a tool, not a replacement for human creativity. It can enhance, extend, and challenge us, but it will never replicate the embodied experience that defines us as creators.

Conclusion: A Journey, Not an Answer

  • Wrap up by reminding the reader that this is a journey of discovery. You don’t have all the answers, but that’s the beauty of it. We’re living in a time when the lines between human and machine are being redrawn, and it’s up to us to figure out what that means for our creative processes.
  • Leave the reader with a question: How can we continue to embrace the power of AI while holding onto the uniquely human elements that make our art, our work, and our lives meaningful?
