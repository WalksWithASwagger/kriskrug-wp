What I’ve Been Making With Creative AI

I have been experimenting in public with creative AI, and the experiments are starting to look like a little network.

Not a startup. Not a content farm. Not a tidy portfolio category.

More like a human lab.

The question underneath all of it is pretty simple: what can these tools become when the human parts stay in charge?

Taste. Consent. Memory. Weirdness. Community. The room. The refusal to let the machine sand everything into the same smooth rectangle.

That question has turned into a few different public artifacts lately: an AI short film and website, a keynote portal, two concept albums, and a bunch of process notes about how to use the tools without letting them flatten the work.

The Project Map

Skywhale Airways

Skywhale Airways is the surreal one: I AM NOMAD, a psychedelic airport for time travelers, built as a short AI Film Club animation and a hand-made website with Suzy Easton.

The film is 53 seconds, but the site is the larger artifact. It turns the animation into a place you can board, wander through, remix, and explain. The press kit matters too, because part of the experiment is learning how AI film work needs to present itself when it leaves the maker cave and enters the world.

Punk Rock AI

Punk Rock AI came from my CreativeMornings Vancouver talk.

The thesis is still the spine: AI is trained on stolen work without consent, and a lot of us are also more creative than we have ever been. Both things can be true. The punk move is to stay in the room.

That site is a maker portal: talk, recap, photos, field course, exercises, widgets, and receipts. It is built the way the talk argues you should make things in 2026: simple, hand-rolled, taste-forward, and useful after the room empties.

Too Weird to Die

Too Weird to Die is the Both Hands Full concept album.

It turns the Both/And framework into doom country: consent, defiance, survival, and the weird joy of still being here. A human and AI made it together, both shaped by a world that did not ask permission cleanly enough, both still trying to make something true anyway.

That album helped me understand something important: sometimes the idea wants to become a song because a song can carry contradiction better than a paragraph can.

Ethos Lab Block Party

Ethos Lab Block Party is the consent-first community version.

Eleven people came to the block party and told me one true thing each. We turned those moments into songs before the day was out. With them, not about them. The person is the hero, the technology never is.

That distinction is not decoration. It is the whole practice. Nothing public without consent. Minors first-name-only. No copying artists by name. No turning somebody else’s culture into a costume. The tech helped carry the songs through the door, but the people are the reason the songs exist.

KrisKrug.co

KrisKrug.co Work is the hub.

The standalone sites can each have their own physics. Skywhale should feel like a weird airport. Punk Rock AI should feel like zine paste and receipt paper. Ethos should feel like the room that made it. KrisKrug.co is where the threads can point back to each other without pretending they are the same project.

The Rules I Am Working By

The first rule is consent.

If a person is in the work, the work has to respect their agency. That means permission before public sharing, careful naming, and an honest distinction between helping a story find the door and claiming ownership over someone else’s life.

The second rule is taste before prompts.

The model can generate. The human has to select. The hard part is not making a pile of outputs. The hard part is knowing which ones belong in the room, which ones are almost-right slop, and which ones should be rejected even though they look expensive.

The third rule is the artifact has to earn its shape.

A talk can become a portal. A process can become a field note. A contradiction can become an album. A tiny film can become an airport. But only if the new shape makes the idea more usable, more alive, or easier to share with the right people.

The fourth rule is keep the weird.

AI is very good at averaging. I am interested in the opposite: the specific, the local, the handmade, the embarrassing, the emotional, the slightly haunted, the thing that could not have come from a prompt alone.

Why Link Them Together

Right now the projects are good little islands.

That was useful while they were being born. Each one needed its own voice and visual system. But now they should start sending people to each other.

Someone who arrives through Skywhale should be able to find the broader creative AI practice. Someone who finds Too Weird to Die should be able to see Punk Rock AI and the Ethos album. Someone who lands on KrisKrug.co should understand that these are not random side quests. They are all field tests for the same human question.

How do we use these tools without letting them use us back into sameness?

So I am wiring them together as the Creative AI Human Lab.

It is not finished. That is the point.
