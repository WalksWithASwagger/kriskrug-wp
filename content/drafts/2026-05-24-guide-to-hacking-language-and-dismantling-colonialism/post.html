1. Unmasking Colonial Legacies in Our Lingo

Let's get real about the sneaky ways colonialism infiltrates our daily chat:

  • "Tribe" or "native" → Try "Indigenous peoples" or specific nation names
  • "Third World" → Consider "Global South" or "developing countries"
  • "Discover" (as in Columbus "discovered" America) → "Encounter" or "invade"

Historical Context: These terms often stem from a Eurocentric worldview that positioned Western cultures as "civilized" and others as "primitive." By examining and changing this language, we challenge centuries-old power structures.

Pro tip: If it sounds like something from a dusty anthropology textbook or a colonial-era novel, it's time for an upgrade.

2. Defusing the Linguistic Minefield (AKA Ditching War Talk)

Our language is often a battlefield. Let's call a ceasefire:

Instead of:

  • "Target audience" → "Intended audience" or "community we serve"
  • "Killing it" → "Excelling" or "Thriving"
  • "Shoot me an email" → "Send me an email" or "Let's connect digitally"

Why it matters: War metaphors normalize violence and aggression in everyday life. By choosing peaceful alternatives, we cultivate a more harmonious communication style.

3. Inclusive Language: Because Words Should Unite, Not Divide

Get ready for some real talk:

  • Use "folks," "everyone," or "team" instead of "guys"
  • "Person with a disability" > "disabled person" (identity-first language varies by community, so listen and respect individual preferences)
  • Be mindful of idioms that don't translate across cultures

Remember: Language shapes reality. By using inclusive terms, we're actively creating a more welcoming world for everyone.

4. Rethinking Nature Metaphors: Eco-Friendly Linguistics

Tech world, environmentalists, and everyone in between, listen up:

  • "Blacklist/Whitelist" → "Blocklist/Allowlist" or "Denylist/Permitlist"
  • "Master/Slave" → "Primary/Secondary" or "Leader/Follower"

Challenge: Create new metaphors that celebrate nature without exploiting it. How about "as adaptable as a chameleon" instead of "two-faced"?

5. Indigenous Languages: Respect, Revitalization, and Recognition

  • Learn and use original place names. Example: Use "Denali" instead of "Mount McKinley"
  • When borrowing Indigenous terms, provide context and credit. It's not just cool slang; it's living culture

Action step: Research the Indigenous languages of your area. Use and promote them whenever appropriate.
