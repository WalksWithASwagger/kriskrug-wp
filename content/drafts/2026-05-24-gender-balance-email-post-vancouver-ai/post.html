DRAFT Post: The Room Matters: Building a More Inclusive AI Community in Vancouver

I got off a long call with Alexandra Samuel, who’s keynoting our January 28 event. We talked about her talk. We also talked about the room.

Not the venue. Not the lighting. The human room.

Who walks in and feels a little spark of possibility. Who walks in and feels tired before they’ve even found a seat.

Alex told me something I’ve heard in different forms for years, but it hit extra hard this time: walking into AI spaces can feel like time travel for a lot of women. Like we all collectively agreed to cosplay 2010, and nobody asked permission.

That feeling matters. It’s not “vibes.” It’s data your body collects in a second. It’s a rational response to patterns that keep repeating.

I’m writing this because we’re not going to pretend the problem is solved. Also because we’re not going to pretend it’s hopeless. We’re going to treat it like what it is: a design problem with real constraints, real stakes, and real humans on the other side of every decision.

What I’m trying to build

I don’t want Vancouver AI to be another room full of dudes deciding the future.

Not because men are bad. Because monocultures are fragile. Because the future gets baked by whoever is in the room when the decisions get normalized. Because “who shows up” turns into “what gets built” faster than anybody wants to admit.

I want a room that looks like Vancouver. Gender-balanced. Culturally diverse. Intergenerational. Beginner-friendly without being condescending. Advanced without being gatekeepy. Curious without being culty.

A room where a woman can walk in without doing that quick scan that every woman knows. A room where nonbinary folks aren’t treated like an “add-on.” A room where people can be skeptical about AI and still belong.

That’s the north star.

What we’ve done so far

We’ve been putting work into this. Real work, not a poster.

  • We structured our board to reflect the values we say out loud.
  • We prioritize women and nonbinary folks on stage.
  • We build partnerships that bring in people who aren’t already hanging around AI circles.
  • We center Indigenous welcome and protocol because we’re on Indigenous land and that matters in practice, not as decoration.
  • We sponsor youth and BIPOC programs when we can, and we keep doing the slow relationship work that actually changes who has access.

For this month’s event, I sourced three women speakers. Not as a “look at me” moment. As a simple statement: this is normal here.

And still, I can hold all of that in one hand and hold this in the other: someone can walk into our room and feel the old imbalance. Both things can be true at the same time.

That’s the tension. That’s the work.

The uncomfortable truth about intent

Intent is invisible.

People don’t experience your intent. They experience the room.

They experience who is on stage. Who is asking questions. Who interrupts. Who gets laughter. Who gets challenged. Who gets ignored. They experience the schedule. The pacing. The noise. The energy. The way networking can turn into a contact sport.

They also experience their own lives. Kids. Caregiving. menopause. chronic fatigue. sensory overload. social anxiety. auditory processing differences. A brain that is brilliant but hates crowded rooms with cross-talk.

If your event design assumes a narrow type of human, you’ll keep getting a narrow type of human. Even if your values are excellent.

What the numbers suggest (with humility)

After Alex and I talked, I did a quick, imperfect analysis using first names only. That method is biased, culturally messy, and not identity-accurate. It does not capture nonbinary identities. It’s a blunt instrument. I used it anyway because sometimes you need a rough signal to stop arguing with yourself.

Here’s what the rough signal suggested:

  • Our ticket buyers for January are close to gender parity.
  • Our core membership, the people who stick around, still skews more male.

If that’s even roughly true, it’s a big clue.

It suggests the event can feel like a “yes” for a lot of women, enough to buy a ticket and show up. But something in the experience, or the follow-through, or the ongoing culture isn’t reliably converting that “yes” into long-term belonging.

That gap is not a moral failure. It’s a design failure. Design failures are solvable.

Also, we need better data. Name-guessing is a hack, not a strategy. More on that below.

Constraints, because reality exists

I want to be transparent about what’s fixed and what’s flexible.

We have a year-long contract with our venue. Time slots are not infinitely adjustable. This isn’t me being precious about tradition. It’s a scheduling reality.

Also, tickets pay for the room. If we don’t sell tickets, the event doesn’t happen. I’m not sitting on a big surplus. This is community infrastructure held together by attention, relationships, and a spreadsheet that is always one surprise away from chaos.

So yes, I’m open to experiments. Also yes, the experiments have to survive physics.

What we’re testing next

We’re treating this like a laboratory. Not in a cold way. In a respectful way. If we care about outcomes, we have to test, measure, and iterate. We have to stop relying on gut feelings and isolated anecdotes. Those are useful, but they are not enough.

Here are the experiments we’re actively running or planning:

1) Make the schedule legible and kinder

A lot of people don’t avoid events because they hate learning. They avoid events because the time window feels endless.

So we’re framing nights more clearly: main program has a beginning, middle, and end you can trust. After that, it becomes optional hang time.

If you need to leave at 8:45 to do bedtime or to protect your nervous system, you should still feel like you got the full meal.

2) Targeted invites through trusted networks

Instead of awkward public pricing that turns gender into a coupon code, we’re piloting small invite pools shared through trusted relationships.

This is not about charity. It’s about changing first impressions. One night where the room feels balanced can change what people believe is possible, and whether they come back.

3) Partnerships that bring in whole communities

Discounts through partner organizations are one of the cleanest tools we have. They lower financial risk and signal welcome without turning identity into a marketing campaign.

If you run or belong to an org that should be in the room, tell me. We can build something that actually works.

4) Speaker pipeline, not speaker shopping

If you want consistent representation, you don’t “find a woman speaker” each month like it’s a scavenger hunt. You build a pipeline.

That means: relationship-building, topic development, support, predictable slots, and a culture where new voices feel held, not thrown into the deep end.

5) Neurodivergent-friendly improvements

This is bigger than gender, and it intersects with gender constantly.

We’re exploring:

  • clearer agendas
  • predictable pacing
  • better mic discipline
  • less chaotic cross-talk
  • a quiet corner where you can reset
  • a culture where it’s normal to take breaks

If the room is only usable for the most extroverted nervous systems, we will keep losing incredible people.

6) Measure retention, not just attendance

If people show up once and then disappear, that’s a signal.

We need a better onboarding path from “attendee” to “member” to “regular” that doesn’t require aggressive networking or social stamina.

Better data, the ethical way

I don’t want to keep guessing identity from names. It’s inaccurate and it’s not respectful.

We’re going to add optional self-described demographics in a lightweight way, including nonbinary identities and “prefer not to say.” No pressure. No weirdness. Just a way to see patterns honestly.

If we’re going to talk about inclusion, we should be willing to measure it.

What I’m asking from you

If you’ve read this far, you probably care. Good. I do too.

Reply with a line or two. One sentence is fine. You don’t need to write an essay.

  1. If you’re a woman or nonbinary person: what makes an AI event feel like a yes or a no?
  2. If you’ve bounced off AI spaces before: what would make you try again?
  3. If you’re a regular: who’s missing, and what would actually get them through the door?
  4. Bonus, especially for neurodivergent folks: what would make the space easier to participate in?

Also: who should we partner with to reach the people missing from the room?

Accountability

We’re going to publish what we try and what changes. Not as a victory lap. As shared learning.

If we’re serious, we should be willing to say:

  • what we did
  • what happened
  • what we’re doing next

No shame. No posturing. Just honest iteration.

Gratitude, and a little sass

I’m grateful Alex brought this to me directly. It’s not always easy to be the person who names the thing everyone sees but nobody wants to say out loud.

I’m also grateful to the people who show up anyway. Especially the people who show up despite feeling like they are walking into a room that wasn’t designed with them in mind. That costs something.

We can do better. We will do better. Not by declaring it. By designing it.

See you under the dome,

Kris Krüg

Blog post draft

Designing the room

I’ve been thinking a lot about a specific moment at events.

It’s not the applause. It’s not the Q&A. It’s the first ten minutes, when someone walks in, scans the room, and their body makes a decision before their brain finishes the sentence.

Yes, this is for me.

No, this is not worth my time.

Maybe, but I’m going to keep my guard up.

That moment is where communities either grow or calcify.

The thing we don’t say out loud

AI spaces, even “good intent” AI spaces, can feel like time travel for women. Same for a lot of nonbinary folks. Same for people who have learned the hard way that “networking” sometimes means “performing calm while you’re being sized up.”

You can be running a thoughtful event with a solid program and still accidentally ship a room that reads as: male, inside-baseball, status-y, and socially risky. Nobody has to be a villain for that to happen. The room itself becomes the message.

What we’re doing, concretely

Vancouver AI is not trying to be a club. We’re trying to be a commons.

That means we have to design for who feels welcome, who feels safe enough to be curious, and who feels like their intelligence will be met with respect instead of being tested.

Some things we’re already doing:

  • Governance that reflects the community we say we want.
  • Speaker curation that prioritizes women and nonbinary folks as a default, not as a theme night.
  • Partnerships that bring new networks in, not just the usual suspects.
  • Sponsorship support for youth and BIPOC programs, because access compounds.

This month, I’ve already sourced three women speakers. I’m proud of that, and I’m also not interested in treating it like a trophy. It’s a corrective. It’s normal. It’s overdue.

The data that keeps me honest

I pulled our Luma exports for the past year: transactions and feedback.

Important caveat: we do not collect self-identified gender in ticketing, and we shouldn’t pretend we can infer identity perfectly from a name. So what follows is “name-associated gender,” not truth about anyone’s life.

Even with that limitation, the trend is loud:

  • Among paying emails where the email looks like a real first name, roughly one third read as women and two thirds read as men.
  • Among feedback voices where we can infer men/women, women are closer to one quarter.

Here’s the part that surprised me, and it matters.

Once someone is already in the paying base, women and men leave feedback at about the same rate. The gap is not mainly “women won’t speak up.” The gap is earlier. It’s who gets into the pipeline in the first place.

Also, women who do leave written feedback tend to write longer, more detailed comments. That reads to me like higher signal and higher care. If you are one of those people, thank you. You’re doing invisible labor that improves the whole system.

So what’s the actual problem?

It’s not a single problem. It’s a stack.

  • Invitation networks skew male unless you deliberately counter-skew.
  • Speaker lineups signal who belongs, even before the first slide.
  • Topic framing can accidentally reward posturing over learning.
  • Room dynamics can punish people who are new, different, or tired of proving they exist.
  • Micro-moments decide whether someone comes back: the greeting, the first conversation, the way questions are handled, the way jokes land.

If you’ve never felt unwelcome in a technical room, you can miss how quickly a room can become a filter. Not because anyone said “no girls allowed,” but because the social cost of being there is too high.

What we’re committing to

Not vibes. Not statements. Design.

  1. A stage that reflects the future we claim to want. Women and nonbinary speakers are not a quota. They’re expertise that has been underbooked.
  2. A room that doesn’t punish beginners. Curiosity is a feature, not a weakness.
  3. A community that doesn’t outsource inclusion to the people least served by the current setup. If you’re a regular, this is on you too.
  4. Measurement with humility. If we care, we track. If we track, we improve.

Next step on measurement is straightforward: we need attendance check-in data, not just purchases and feedback. Otherwise we’re guessing at “who shows up.” I’d rather know.

What I’m asking from you

This is the part where I shut up and listen.

If you’re a woman or nonbinary person:

  • What makes an AI event feel like a yes?
  • What makes it feel like a no?

If you’ve bounced off AI spaces before:

  • What specifically pushed you away?

If you’re a regular:

  • Who is missing?
  • What would actually get them through the door?

Specific beats general here. “Make it inclusive” is a wish. “Have a host introduce me to two people and keep it normal” is a design spec.

Why I’m being this direct

Because AI is shaping the next decade of work, creativity, and power. If the room is skewed, the future gets skewed. It’s not abstract.

And also because I’ve seen what happens when a room gets it right. People relax. People share. People learn faster. The community becomes smarter, not louder.

That’s the room I want under the dome.

No perfection. No purity tests. Just a steady commitment to building something worth walking into.

See you there,

Kris
