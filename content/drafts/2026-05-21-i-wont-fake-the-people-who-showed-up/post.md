I can generate a beautiful fake person in fourteen seconds.

Perfect light. Symmetrical face. Heroic posture. Wardrobe that somehow knows the assignment better than the client did. No blink. No nerves. No need for parking, coffee, consent forms, weather backup, or the awkward moment where the flash does not fire and everyone pretends not to notice.

Amazing.

Still not a portrait.

A portrait is not just an image of a person. It is a record of attention between people. It is a little contract of trust. It says: you stood there, I stood here, something happened between us, and this is the trace.

That trace matters more now, not less.

In the synthetic age, the fact that someone actually showed up becomes part of the value of the work.

So here is one of my lines:

I will use AI around documentary work.

I will not use AI to fake the people who showed up.

## Documentary Work Is Not A Pixel Problem

The dumbest version of the AI conversation treats all images as output.

Image goes in. Image comes out. Prompt better. Generate faster. Save money. Ship more.

That worldview makes sense if you think photography is only a production step.

I do not.

I come from documentary photography, community media, event documentation, festival chaos, artist portraits, hallway conversations, and all the weird little human moments that do not fit inside a shot list.

The job is not "make picture."

The job is witness.

Sometimes the job is to make someone feel safe enough to be seen. Sometimes it is to notice who keeps getting left out of the frame. Sometimes it is to catch the moment where a room stops performing and starts telling the truth. Sometimes it is to build an archive so a community can remember itself later.

AI can generate a plausible image of a community.

It cannot be accountable to that community.

It cannot remember who was nervous before the talk. It cannot ask the elder how they want their name spelled. It cannot feel the room shift when the welcome song starts. It cannot know that the person in the back corner is the one who built half the thing but will never step toward the microphone unless someone notices.

That is not nostalgia. That is the actual work.

## What AI Can Do Around The Frame

None of this means "keep the machines out."

Please. I am not trying to turn documentary practice into a candlelit museum of analog purity.

AI can be wildly useful around the frame.

It can help me search thirty years of images when my memory has the file but not the filename.

It can help draft captions from notes.

It can turn a transcript into a clip list.

It can pull recurring themes from interviews.

It can help build contact sheets for a client.

It can organize community names, projects, roles, and relationships so the archive becomes a living map instead of a hard drive full of ghosts.

It can help generate a shot list before an event, identify missing proof after an event, and turn the whole thing into a useful story package that sponsors, organizers, participants, and future collaborators can actually understand.

That is all fair game when the consent, privacy, and context are right.

That is the art-adjacent layer.

Use the machine there. Let it carry boxes. Let it sort the cables. Let it find the quote. Let it rename the folders. Let it help the human witness travel farther.

But do not confuse the logistics around witness with the witness itself.

## The Line I Draw

Here is the line in plain language.

I will not replace a portrait of a community member with an AI-generated portrait of a community member.

I will not create fake documentary evidence of a gathering.

I will not invent attendance, diversity, intimacy, consensus, ceremony, joy, or grief because a deck needs a stronger hero image.

I will not let "close enough" become the standard for human truth.

That last one is where the danger lives.

Synthetic media trains people to accept plausible surfaces. Looks like a person. Looks like a crowd. Looks like a community. Looks like care.

But documentary work is not about looking like care.

It is care with a shutter attached.

When organizations use synthetic people to represent real communities, they are not just saving money. They are teaching themselves that people are interchangeable symbols. They are practicing extraction even when the image looks warm and inclusive and brand-safe.

That is how trust leaks out of a culture.

Quietly. Pixel by pixel.

## Presence Is The New Provenance

The word "provenance" gets thrown around a lot in AI conversations now. Usually it means source data, model lineage, watermarking, chain of custody.

Good. We need that.

But documentary practice has always had another kind of provenance:

Were you there?

Did they know why you were there?

Did you ask?

Did you listen?

Did you spell the name right?

Did you send the photo back?

Did the people represented get any value from the representation?

Did the archive become a commons or a trophy case?

In the next few years, that kind of provenance is going to matter more than ever.

Not because every photo needs to become a legal deposition. Because the internet is about to be flooded with images that never touched a life.

When everything can be generated, the lived trace becomes precious.

The proof that someone stood somewhere, with other people, in a room, on a territory, inside a particular weather system of relationships, will become part of the meaning.

Presence becomes provenance.

## Questions For Teams Before They Replace Documentation

If you are an event producer, funder, nonprofit, school, studio, or community organization thinking about using AI-generated imagery instead of documentation, slow down for ten minutes and ask better questions.

What job is this image doing?

Is it illustrative, conceptual, speculative, or documentary?

Will anyone reasonably understand it as proof that something happened?

Are we representing real people, real communities, real cultural practices, or real relationships?

Who benefits if we fake it?

Who loses visibility if we do?

What would happen if we hired someone from the community to document it instead?

Could AI help us prepare, organize, caption, archive, and distribute the real documentation rather than replace it?

If the answer is "we just need a vibe," say that out loud.

Sometimes a vibe is fine. Use abstract imagery. Use illustration. Use clearly labeled speculative art. Use weird generative textures until the walls melt.

But if the image is standing in for real human presence, treat it like a promise.

And do not make promises with fake people.

## Both Hands Full, Again

This is the same old contradiction, because apparently this moment has decided to make contradiction its primary operating system.

AI is built on a messy pile of consent problems, power problems, labor problems, and cultural problems.

AI is also useful.

Both things are true.

So I am not walking away from the tools. I am also not handing them the sacred center of my practice and calling that adaptation.

I want AI in the archive.

I want AI in the notes.

I want AI helping me find the forgotten story, surface the buried pattern, draft the first pass, organize the proof, and make the real work easier to share.

I do not want AI replacing the person in front of me.

I do not want synthetic community to stand in for actual community.

I do not want documentary practice turned into visual karaoke for organizations that want the glow of human connection without the responsibility of relationship.

The future is going to be full of generated everything.

Fine.

Then let the real thing get more real.

Let the rooms matter.

Let the people matter.

Let the photographer be more than a content machine.

And if someone trusted us enough to show up, do not fake them after the fact.
